# Task 1: An Introduction to Risk

**Program:** Goldman Sachs – Risk Management Virtual Experience Program (Forage)
**Difficulty:** Easy | **Duration:** 20–30 minutes

## Objective

This task introduces the fundamentals of risk assessment by applying core financial risk categories to everyday, relatable scenarios. The exercise strengthens the ability to identify, categorize, and think critically about risk — a skill directly applicable to a career in risk management.

---

## Part 1: Risk Identification

For each scenario, a range of potential risks were identified across personal, financial, operational, and external factors.

### Scenario 1: Getting to a 9:00 a.m. Monday Exam
- A transit strike affecting public transportation
- A friend forgetting to bring an extra calculator to the exam
- Not having enough balance on a public transport card with no immediate means to top it up
- Misplacing the exam hall ticket
- Misinterpreting the exam timetable

### Scenario 2: Organizing a Weekend Road Trip with Friends
- A friend not paying their share of the trip expenses
- Car breakdown
- Overestimating the car's fuel efficiency
- Fuel prices suddenly increasing
- Running out of money during the trip

### Scenario 3: Planning an On-Campus Event for an Extracurricular Organization
- Failure of audiovisual equipment
- An overlapping major campus event reducing attendance
- A sponsor backing out at the last minute
- Unexpected costs exceeding the budget
- Overestimating attendee numbers based on interest shown on social media

## Understanding the Risk Categories

| Category | Definition | Everyday Interpretation |
|---|---|---|
| **Market Risk** | The risk of loss arising from external, uncontrollable factors in the broader environment, such as price fluctuations, competition, or macroeconomic changes. | An outside factor beyond one's control disrupts the plan (e.g., traffic, price changes, a competing event). |
| **Credit Risk** | The risk that a counterparty — an individual, organization, or institution one depends on — fails to fulfill their financial or contractual obligation. | Someone you were relying on doesn't follow through on their commitment. |
| **Liquidity Risk** | The risk that an entity will not have sufficient cash or liquid assets available to meet its short-term obligations when needed. | Money or a resource isn't available at the exact moment it's required. |
| **Operational Risk** | The risk of loss resulting from inadequate or failed internal processes, systems, human error, or equipment failure. | Something within your own control (a system, process, or piece of equipment) breaks down or is mismanaged. |
| **Model Risk** | The risk of loss arising from decisions based on incorrect or flawed models, assumptions, or estimates used during planning and forecasting. | A plan is built on an inaccurate assumption or estimate, leading to an incorrect outcome. |

## Key Learnings

- **Market Risk** arises from external, uncontrollable factors in the environment (e.g., strikes, price fluctuations, competing events).
- **Credit Risk** arises when a counterparty (a person or organization one depends on) fails to meet their commitment.
- **Liquidity Risk** arises when funds or resources are not available when needed.
- **Operational Risk** arises from internal process failures, human error, or system/equipment breakdowns.
- **Model Risk** arises from flawed assumptions or inaccurate estimations used in planning.

This exercise demonstrated that the same risk categorization framework used in professional financial risk management can be applied to everyday planning and decision-making, reinforcing the practical relevance of structured risk assessment.
